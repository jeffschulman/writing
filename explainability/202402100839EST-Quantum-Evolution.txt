Introduction
In 1935, Albert Einstein and Erwin Schr√∂dinger, two of the most prominent physicists of the day, got into a dispute over the nature of reality.

Einstein had done the math and knew that the universe must be local, meaning that no event in one location could instantly affect a distant location. But Schr√∂dinger had done his own math, and he knew that at the heart of quantum mechanics lay a strange connection he dubbed ‚Äúentanglement,‚Äù which appeared to strike at Einstein‚Äôs commonsense assumption of locality.

When two particles become entangled, which can happen when they collide, their fates become linked. Measure the orientation of one particle, for instance, and you may learn that its entangled partner (if and when it is measured) points in the opposite direction, no matter its location. Thus, a measurement in Beijing could appear to instantly affect an experiment in Brooklyn, apparently violating Einstein‚Äôs edict that no influence can travel faster than light.

Einstein disliked the reach of entanglement (which he would later refer to as ‚Äúspooky‚Äù) and criticized the then-nascent theory of quantum mechanics as necessarily incomplete. Schr√∂dinger in turn defended the theory, which he had helped pioneer. But he sympathized with Einstein‚Äôs distaste for entanglement. Physicists have since largely shed that discomfort. They now understand what Einstein, and perhaps Schr√∂dinger himself, had overlooked ‚Äî that entanglement has no remote influence. Entanglement experiments, such as those that won the 2022 Nobel Prize, have now grown routine.

Over the last few years, a flurry of theoretical and experimental research has uncovered a strange new face of the phenomenon ‚Äî one that shows itself not in pairs, but in constellations of particles. ‚ÄúThis is a phase transition in information,‚Äù said Brian Skinner of Ohio State University, one of the physicists who first identified the phenomenon. ‚ÄúIt‚Äôs where the properties in information ‚Äî how information is shared between things ‚Äî undergo a very abrupt change.‚Äù

First, a bit of a rewind. To understand what entanglement has to do with information, imagine a pair of particles, A and B, each with a spin that can be measured as pointing up or down. Each particle begins in a quantum superposition of up and down, meaning that a measurement produces a random outcome ‚Äî either up or down. There‚Äôs no way to make a physicist happier than to show that a problem that looks hard is actually equivalent to a problem you already know how to solve.

How much a measurement of B reduces your ignorance about A is called the entanglement entropy, and like any type of information, it is counted in bits. Entanglement entropy is the main way physicists quantify the entanglement between two objects, or, equivalently, how much information about one is stored nonlocally in the other. No entanglement entropy means no entanglement; measuring B reveals nothing about A. High entanglement entropy means a lot of entanglement; measuring B teaches you a lot about A.

Over dessert, Skinner and Nahum took this thinking two steps further. They first extended the pair of particles into a chain as long as one cared to imagine. They knew that according to Schr√∂dinger‚Äôs eponymous equation, the quantum mechanics analog of F = ma, entanglement would jump from one particle to the next like the flu. Measuring half the spins will give you a good idea of what to expect when you measure the other half.

Next, they moved measurement from the end of the process ‚Äî when the particle chain had already reached a particular quantum state ‚Äî to the middle of the action, while entanglement was spreading. Doing so created a conflict because measurement is the mortal enemy of entanglement. Left untouched, the quantum state of a group of particles reflects all the possible combinations of ups and downs you might get when you measure those particles. Nahum asked Skinner the following question: What if, while entanglement was in the process of spreading, you measured some of the spins here and there? Constantly measuring them all would snuff out all the entanglement in a boring way. But if you sporadically measured just a few spins, which phenomenon would emerge victorious?
